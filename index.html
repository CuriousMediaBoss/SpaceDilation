Definition

I define Space Dilation as the phenomenon by which a measurement perpendicular to an observer's line of sight — of true magnitude H₀ — is observed to have apparent magnitude H_obs = H₀ cos θ, where θ is the angle subtended between the perpendicular axis and the line connecting observer to object. The observed fraction satisfies sin²θ + (H_obs/H₀)² = 1. I distinguish two distances: the observed distance D (the projected horizontal separation, bounded) and the proper distance D/cos θ (the true line-of-sight distance, unbounded).

Isomorphism with Length Contraction

Lorentz length contraction states that the observed length of an object moving at coordinate speed v is L = L₀√(1 − v²/c²), satisfying (v/c)² + (L/L₀)² = 1. The coordinate speed v (bounded by c) and the proper velocity γv (unbounded) stand in the same relationship as observed distance D and proper distance D/cos θ: the proper quantity is the observed quantity scaled by the same factor (γ = 1/cos θ) that governs the spatial compression. Both systems are described by the single relation f(x) = 1/√(1 + x²), where x is the normalized proper quantity and f is the observed fraction.

Space-Time Coupling

In each system, the arc intersection encodes a spatial effect and a temporal effect simultaneously, determined by the same angle θ. The character of the temporal coupling differs between the two systems. In length contraction, time dilation is multiplicative: the moving object's clock rate is reduced by a factor of 1/γ, where γ is read directly from the arc. In space dilation, the temporal effect is additive: the observation is displaced D/c seconds into the past — a look-back time that is itself determined by the same arc intersection that sets the spatial scaling. In both cases, a single geometric quantity (the arc angle θ) simultaneously determines what happens to the observed spatial measurement and what happens to time.

Proposal

I propose that Space Dilation is not a separate geometric phenomenon that happens to share the mathematics of Lorentz Length Contraction — it is the same phenomenon, expressed without velocity across static spatial separation. Length contraction is difficult to observe directly: it requires relative velocities approaching c, which lie far outside ordinary experience. Space Dilation, I conjecture, is the same structural effect with no velocity term — observable in everyday perception whenever a measurement perpendicular to the line of sight is viewed at a distance. I am not proposing an analogy. I am proposing that this equivalence reveals an intricate relationship between static space and motion: the same arc that governs what happens to measurement under relativistic velocity also governs what happens to measurement under spatial separation alone, with no motion required. If correct, this suggests that the relativistic structure of observation is not exclusive to motion — it is a property of space itself.

I further note a striking asymmetry in how each domain treats time. When motion is present, time is attenuated — the moving object's clock runs at a reduced rate (1/γ < 1), and that rate diminishes further as velocity increases. Motion appears to act against the passage of time, progressively suppressing it as the arc angle grows. When motion is absent, as in Space Dilation, time is not attenuated at all — it is merely displaced. The observer receives the same undiminished flow of time, offset by D/c into the past. This distinction — between time that is diminished by motion and time that is shifted by distance — may itself be significant: it suggests that motion and static spatial separation engage with time in fundamentally different ways, even as they produce identical spatial compressions under the same arc.

This asymmetry connects to a deeper conservation principle. In special relativity, every object moves through spacetime at a fixed total rate — the speed of light c — partitioned between time and space. A stationary object expends its full allocation on the passage of time. As velocity increases, that allocation transfers progressively to spatial motion, reducing the rate of proper time accordingly. At v = c, the transfer is complete: the entire allocation has become motion, and proper time ceases. The arc encodes this conservation exactly: cos θ = 1/γ is the fraction of the allocation remaining in time; sin θ = v/c is the fraction that has become motion. Their squares sum to unity — nothing is created or lost, only redistributed.

Under this interpretation, Space Dilation occupies a distinct position: it produces the same spatial compression as length contraction while leaving the temporal allocation entirely intact. Static spatial separation does not draw from the spacetime budget — it compresses measurement without consuming any of time's force. I conjecture that this reveals something fundamental about the relationship between space and motion: that the arc governing relativistic observation under velocity is the same arc governing spatial observation at rest, but the two domains make entirely different demands on time. One redistributes it. The other leaves it whole.

To my knowledge, this identification has not been previously proposed in the literature.
